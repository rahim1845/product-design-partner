## 1. What reviewers scan for (in roughly this order, in the first 60–90 seconds)

1. **Can they think?** Problem framing before solution — was the right problem chosen, and why?
2. **Can they decide?** Tradeoffs named, options rejected with reasons. A case study with no rejected options reads as a render gallery.
3. **Did it work?** Outcomes, honestly reported — including what didn't move.
4. **Can they craft?** Visual/interaction quality, but as evidence of judgment, not decoration.
5. **Would I want them in the room?** Voice, collaboration signals, how they handled constraints and disagreement.

Three deep case studies beat eight shallow ones. Each should answer one question a hiring manager has ("can this person handle ambiguity / systems / metrics / zero-to-one?") — pick case studies to cover the question set of the *target role*, not to inventory everything done.

## 2. Case study anatomy (narrative arc, not process documentation)

A strong case study is a story with rising tension, not a chronological process log. Workable arc (Freytag-compatible):

1. **Hook:** the outcome or the tension in one screen ("we had 40k orders and an ops team drowning").
2. **Context:** product, role, constraints, team — brief. State exactly what *you* did vs the team.
3. **The problem behind the problem:** the reframe — what discovery revealed that the brief got wrong.
4. **Climax decision(s):** the 1–2 hardest calls, with the options considered, the evidence, the tradeoff accepted. This is the heart; spend the most space here.
5. **Execution evidence:** key flows/screens that *embody the decision*, not a screen dump.
6. **Outcome:** numbers where real, qualitative signal where not, and what you'd do differently.
7. **Reflection:** one honest lesson. Reads as maturity, not weakness.

Anti-patterns: double-diamond diagrams as filler · persona/journey artifacts shown without the decision they drove · "I conducted user research" with no finding stated · outcomes claimed with no baseline · every project ending in triumph (reviewers distrust a portfolio with no scars).

## 3. Evidence honesty

Proxy-research projects: label them ("synthesized from 40 app store reviews and 12 Reddit threads; next step: 5 interviews"). This *raises* credibility — it shows you know what real evidence is. Never invent quotes, users, or research that didn't happen; experienced reviewers can smell fabricated research in seconds, and one detected fabrication poisons the whole portfolio.

## 4. Metrics in case studies

- Use verified numbers only; round honestly; give the baseline ("activation 34% → 41%" not "+21% improvement" stacked on an unstated base).
- Attribute fairly: "the team shipped X; my contribution was the Y flow, which drove…" — claiming team outcomes as solo work is the most common senior-review red flag.
- No metric? Use qualitative outcomes (support tickets stopped, stakeholder decision unblocked, time-to-task observed in testing) — honest qualitative beats inflated quantitative.

## 5. Presenting work (live walkthroughs, panel reviews)

Use the narrative tools from narrative-and-writing.md: what-is/what-could-be for the setup; one headline per project ("the decision that mattered was X"); show the artifact only after the tension is established. Time-box: a portfolio presentation slot is usually 30–45 min for 2–3 projects — rehearse the cut, don't improvise it. End each project on the outcome + lesson, not on a screen.

## 6. Product-sense answers (design/PM interviews)

Structure a spoken answer the way the skill structures real work:

1. Clarify the goal + constraints (30 seconds of questions is expected, not weakness).
2. Pick a segment and *say why* (pain × reachability — see product-strategy §1).
3. Name the user's job and top pain points; prioritize one with a stated reason.
4. Sketch 2–3 solution directions; pick one with the tradeoff named.
5. Define success: one primary metric + one guardrail.
6. Name the riskiest assumption and how you'd cheaply test it.

The evaluator is scoring the *shape of your reasoning* — explicit assumptions, prioritization with reasons, comfort with ambiguity — more than the specific idea.
