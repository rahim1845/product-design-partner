## 1. UX writing fundamentals

- Button verbs describe outcome: "Create account" over "Submit" — except where submitting is the literal user-understood act (tax filings, legal consent, applications).
- Error messages: what happened → why → what to do next, in that order. Bad: "Error 402. Invalid input." Good: "We couldn't charge that card. Check the number and expiration date, or try another card."
- Empty states orient and invite the next action; celebratory states can play, failure states stay calm and specific, never cute.
- Onboarding: one idea per screen; teach by doing; defer everything non-essential.
- Review copy by walking the flow, not inspecting strings — a label only works in the sentence of its screen.

## 2. Reading level (Flesch Reading Ease)

| Target | FRE | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Grade 5–6 | 80–90 | Mass-market consumer, children |
| Grade 7–8 | 60–70 | **Default** general-audience products |
| Grade 9–10 | 50–60 | Professional tools, B2B SaaS |
| Grade 11+ | <50 | Technical/scientific/legal only |

Run copy through a readability checker before ship; if it misses target, cut words and shorten sentences.

## 3. Voice & tone calibration (1–5 per axis; makes voice testable)

Axes: Formal↔Casual · Serious↔Playful · Matter-of-fact↔Enthusiastic · Reverent↔Irreverent.

Real-product coordinates (same category, different positions = measurable differentiation):

| Product | F↔C | S↔P | M↔E | R↔I |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Vanguard | 5 | 1 | 2 | 1 |
| Mint | 3 | 3 | 3 | 2 |
| Robinhood | 2 | 4 | 4 | 4 |
| Duolingo | 2 | 5 | 5 | 5 |

Voice is constant; tone flexes by context (empty state 5/5 playful; failed payment 2/2).

## 4. Selling the transformation

Jobs didn't sell a 5GB MP3 player; he sold "1,000 songs in your pocket." The job is what people buy.

**Word reduction as craft (Strunk & White):** cut every word whose removal costs no meaning; concrete beats abstract ("loads in 2 seconds" > "fast"); if you can't say it in six words you don't know it yet.

v1: "Our advanced AI-powered platform helps freelancers leverage habit formation science to optimize productivity workflows."
v2: "Freelancers use our app to build habits that stick."
v3: "Habits that stick, for people who work alone."

**Value prop template:** For [specific user] who [job/pain], [product] is [category] that [key benefit unlike alternatives]. Anchor in the positioning chain from product-strategy.md §2 — value props written without positioning are slogans.

## 5. Presentation structure

**Duarte's what-is / what-could-be (Resonate):** establish today's reality → contrast the wanted future → alternate to build tension → end in the "new bliss." Behind Jobs keynotes, "I Have a Dream," most sticky TED talks. Use for launches, pitch decks, board readouts.

**Jobs keynote anatomy (Gallo):** villain (the shared bad experience) → hero (the product) → headline ≤140 chars → three points → live demo (show, don't tell) → planted surprise.

**PR/FAQ (Amazon, Working Backwards):** write the press release before building; if the headline isn't compelling, the product isn't ready. Template in templates.md.

## 6. Naming

Good names are sensory, surprising, or outcome-describing (Dropbox, Slack, Stripe). Bad names describe the technology (RealtimeFileSync v2). Test: say the name to five non-target people, ask what it does; if two or more guess wrong in a damaging way, keep working.

## 7. Landing page anatomy

| Element | Rule |
|---|---|
| Headline | One-sentence transformation, not the product |
| Subhead | Who it's for + what they get |
| Primary CTA | Verb + outcome ("Start my first habit" > "Sign up") |
| Social proof | Near the CTA, not buried |
| Hero visual | The *after* state, not UI chrome |
| Second CTA | Same action, different words, further down |

## 8. Pitfalls

Feature listing as value prop · "we" voice instead of "you" · corporate neutrality (invisible copy) · jargon hiding imprecision · cute over clear · copy written in isolation from the flow.
