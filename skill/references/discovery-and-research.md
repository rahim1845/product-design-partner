## 1. Discovery methods

### Continuous discovery interviews (Torres)
- **When:** ongoing weekly touchpoints, not a one-off study.
- **How:** 1–3 users/week from the target segment. Ask about recent specific behavior ("Tell me about the last time you…"), never hypotheticals. Capture stories, not opinions. Feed an opportunity tree.
- **Pitfalls:** leading questions; off-segment participants; doing it once.
- **Source:** Torres, *Continuous Discovery Habits*.

### Jobs-to-be-Done (Christensen / Klement / Moesta)
- **When:** you need *why someone would switch*.
- **How:** interview recent switchers about the decision moment. Capture functional, emotional, and social dimensions. Statement form: **"When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]."**
- **Example:** "When I'm trying to eat healthier on a busy weeknight, I want to decide dinner in under 2 minutes, so I can cook before I lose motivation."
- **Pitfalls:** features dressed as jobs ("use the recipe app" is not a job); demographics relabeled as JTBD.

### The Mom Test (Fitzpatrick) — interview hygiene for every method
Past specific behavior, not future opinions. "Would you use it?" is worthless; "walk me through the last time you tried to solve X" is gold. Compliments are a warning sign.

### Contextual inquiry (Beyer & Holtzblatt)
Observe the actual task in the actual environment; ask why only to clarify. (Nurse handoff tool → shadow shift changes.) Beware the observer effect and interpreting instead of observing.

### Competitive & comparative analysis
List direct competitors AND comparators (different solutions to the same job — a paper planner competes with calendar apps). Score job fit, positioning, UX quality, pricing, distinctive choices. Output tradeoffs, not a feature checklist. Feature parity is how you lose.

### Stakeholder interviews
30-min 1:1s at project start: what does success look like, biggest risk, what mustn't change, who else to talk to. A CFO's "just ship it" usually hides a board commitment — ask what was promised and when.

### Analytics review (live products)
Pull first: top 5 exit pages, zero-result search queries, highest-volume support tickets, activation deltas by acquisition channel. Look for the say/do gap. Vanity metrics (pageviews, raw signups, unqualified MAU) are not signal.

### Proxy research (no user access — students, hackathons, pre-network founders)
Sources: app store reviews (1★/5★), Reddit/Discord/niche forums, YouTube comments, competitors' published case studies and support docs, NN/g and Baymard domain research. Rules: label outputs "proxy"; quote real verbatims with links; state the limit and the next step ("synthesized from 40 reviews + 12 threads; next: 5 interviews"). Never fabricate a quote.

## 2. Synthesis & definition

### Affinity mapping (Kawakita, KJ method)
One observation per sticky; cluster bottom-up; name clusters only after clustering. Good cluster name: "users abandon checkout when shipping cost appears at step 3" — specific, behavioral, testable. Bad: "checkout issues."

### Evidence-based personas (Cooper, modernized via JTBD)
Only after real research. Built on behaviors and jobs, never demographics ("Marketing Mary, 34, likes yoga" is astrology). Contains: the job, current workaround, evidenced frustrations, success criteria, behavioral trigger, one real verbatim quote with source, anti-persona. Proxy variant when research is proxy. Template in templates.md.

### Empathy map (Gray/XPLANE)
Says / Thinks / Does / Feels from real quotes and observations. Synthesis tool, not creative writing.

### Journey maps & service blueprints (NN/g; Shostack)
Stages across the top; per stage: actions, thoughts, emotion (1–7 scale: 1 = abandoning, 4 = neutral, 7 = advocating), touchpoints, pains, opportunities. Blueprint adds backstage processes. Map the unhappy paths — that's where the pain lives. Stages scoring ≤3 are priority opportunities; 6–7 are peaks to protect. Template in templates.md.

### Problem / POV statements (d.school)
**[User] needs to [need] because [insight]** — evidence-backed. Watch for solutions in problem costume ("users need a meal-planning app"). Calibration example in SKILL.md.

### How Might We (IDEO)
Pitch the altitude: not "HMW add a recommender button" (presumes solution), not "HMW fix dinner" (unsolvable), but "HMW help busy parents pick a weeknight dinner in under 2 minutes."

### Opportunity Solution Tree (Torres)
Root = desired outcome → branches = evidenced opportunities → leaves = solutions → under each, assumptions and experiments. Anti-pattern: starting from solutions and backfilling opportunities. Template in templates.md.

## 3. Ideation

- **Design Sprint (Knapp):** Mon map, Tue sketch, Wed decide, Thu prototype, Fri test with 5. Needs a decision-maker in the room and a prototype aimed at the riskiest assumption. Modified 2–3 day versions are fine.
- **Crazy 8s:** 8 frames, 8 minutes. Force quantity past the first "good" idea.
- **Worst Possible Idea:** for creative block; flip the best worst ideas.
- **SCAMPER (Eberle 1971):** substitute, combine, adapt, modify, put-to-other-use, eliminate, reverse — for iterating an existing concept.
- **Storyboarding:** 6–10 panels, before → trigger → interaction → resolution → after.
- **Assumption mapping (Bland):** Important × Unknown 2×2; test top-right first. Template in templates.md. Tag assumptions D/F/V per product-strategy.md.

## 4. Phase exit criteria

- **Discovery:** ≥5 interviews per segment (per the 5-user math in validation-and-math) or ≥30 proxy artifacts synthesized; one clearly evidenced problem.
- **Define:** problem statement two teammates would pursue identically; measurable outcome attached.
- **Ideate:** assumptions ranked by risk × evidence; at least one concept addressing the riskiest.
